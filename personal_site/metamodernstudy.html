Kiosk: Metamodern Media

An interactive kiosk that makes academic research fun and engaging.

Live Site

Tools

Figma, HTML/CSS, JavaScript, Chrome Kiosk App

Purpose

Metamodernism describes a new cultural philosophy for the digital age. My class assignment was to break down this abstract theory into an interactive experience.

Design Goals

Visual clarity and ease of usability were essential, since the kiosk should be able to be interacted with and understood by anyone.

Time

2 weeks

Process

Context

Metamodernism is a theory that attempts to describe how the digital age thinks and feels about culture. Prior to development, I conducted academic research for a month to better understand the different facets of this idea. Ultimately, I decided that showcasing different media artifacts would be the most accessible way to convey information.

Wireframing

In order to create an engaging user experience, my first challenge was to create an intuitive display. Kiosks I looked to for inspiration were all highly visual, showed only what was necessary, and prioritized a simple flow . My initial wireframing aimed to use visual navigation as much as possible, and used modals to reduce information overload.

To grab the user's attention, I included a "select" screen at the start, so that they could immediately go toward what seemed most interesting.

The main content would be the media section, so I fleshed out its interface further. My initial planning broke it down into categories and their examples. Since metamodernism is such a vast concept, this was my strategy to make it more digestable, allowing people to instantly connect with media they might've seen before.

At this point in ideation, however, it became apparent that I had no concept of the media's interactive modals beyond a static description. This was something I'd have to iterate after getting feedback.

Visual Style

For the project's mood and readibility, I wanted to utilize graphic colors and eye-catching designs that were easy to understand. The intent was to communicate the unique subject matter without being too overwhelming.

Since metamodernism combines old philosophies with newer ways of thinking, I liked the idea of contrasting black-and-white photos with colorful shapes. I created the assets in Figma and drew inspiration from the Frutiger Metro aesthetic.

At this point I was one week into the project, and translated my wireframing into a slide presentation for feedback.

Feedback + Iteration

The initial concepts were workshopped by peers and faculty. Though the experience was easy to follow, it didn't do enough to marry function with meaning . I reworked my modal windows to have unique interactive features that made sense for its media type.

I continued to fiddle with the media selection screen, because while some people tapped through it, others didn't realize they could interact with the icons. Since the touch interface of a Kiosk doesn't have hover animations to show clickable items, I added labels to have more explicit instruction, and this seemed to fix the issue.

At this point I was comfortable with my designs, and began development for the live kiosk.

Implementation

Our professor gave us a starter kit for the code, but I wrote everything from scratch. I placed my custom assets into my HTML/CSS layout, while more complex interactivity used JavaScript.

A major constraint for this project was testing the dimensions constantly, in order to ensure it mapped to the kisok display correctly. We were given a testing tool to account for this, but there were still resolution issues with the end result.

The end site was displayed using the Chrome Kiosk App. My project was selected to be shown as the first kiosk in a week-long showcase, and each person coming across it expressed delight at the interactions and seeing what they could tap on. An article is currently being written about it!

Overall, I'm satisfied with how everything turned out, and I was able to accomplish packaging dense content into a simple, engaging interface.

Takeaways

  • While users enjoyed exploring different features, there was less incentive to read through the text. To encourage learning it would have been useful to have something like a quiz.

  • Designing for touch interfaces demands clear visual indicators and explicit instructions.

  • If I revisited Metamodern Media , I would redo the code to be cleaner, make everything responsive, add more items of interest, and perform more extensive user testing.
