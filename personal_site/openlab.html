Site Redesign: Open Lab

Revamping the website for University of Pittsburgh's 3D makerspace.

Tools

Figma, Airtable

Role

UX/UI Designer

Problem

In order to find out about Open Lab online, new potential members were originally being redirected to a site that was confusing to use. I was tasked with creating a design that would resolve its issues.

Time

5 days

Process

Identifying Problems

When the original website for Open Lab was created, its final result was delivered without consulting any of the staff on the design. As a result, all of the content requirements were there, but the user experience felt awkward to use. Several faculty and student members came in to ask questions at the physical location after being unable to navigate the site. Within the last year, Open Lab has recieved over 3,500 new members, and having a streamlined digital presence will further bolster its accessibility.

To determine what the current pain points were, I spoke to staff and students to determine how they felt about the current layout.

The most apparent issue was a confusing user flow, as Open Lab offers separate services to faculty and students. The original landing page had buttons that immediately led the user away from the site, with one opening Outlook and the other opening a survey.

Another key issue was the amount of redundant content. Multiple pages and menus described all the different equipment of the lab, but the user was already being referred to a Canvas page with the same information. In order to make the site as clean as possible, I would have to provide a very clear general purpose.

In conclusion, the original website wasn't intuitive to use, and it could be simplified to a considerable degree.

Designing for User Needs

The manager wanted me to reduce everything as much as possible, so we decided on using a single page format. I outlined the site's most essential needs:

  • Inform students about how to become a member

  • Offer consultation to faculty

  • Indicate that these were distinct goals

Since the main goal was to delineate the two main user pathways, I replaced the buttons with anchors that offered more description.

The next problem was figuring out how to remove all the content bloat. The lab's manager suggested using only visual icons to display what equipment and services were available. That way, users could see everything at a glance, and it reinforced going to the Canvas page to get more instructive training. The final product would use vector badge icons, which were already available on the internal Airtable for tracking member progress.

With the two main issues reworked, it was time to start translating my initial ideas into the wireframes.

Site Components

As the turnaround was only a few days, I decided on creating modular design components that the developers could implement as they saw best. By doing this, I could quickly and easily provide potential layouts and all of the key content within them.

Working off of the original site's code and styling, I assembled different elements that could be placed anywhere.

With this approach, it was also intuitive to mock up different variations of the sections.

Each anchor would lead to more information, which would then redirect students and faculty to their respective links. This was a more linear experience. Additionally, to still showcase the lab's projects, I included a banner with the Instagram.

Once I had my building blocks in place, I started creating the high fidelity wireframes. First, I constructed a "main" version of the site, demonstrating one potential flow for all of the content. I then added two variations of this layout, providing small changes that didn't impact the overall content, but could offer more stylistic options to the developer.

The design files were approved by the entire staff and sent off for development.

Follow Up

As of now, the revamped website is currently in development.

Following this project, I was approached to redesign another University website that showcases every makerspace on campus. It deals with similar needs and requirements, and I've been using the same methodology to improve user experience.

Takeaways

  • Not communicating past initital requirements leads to a disconnect with the user experience.

  • More is not always better, and information should flow intuitively.

  • Creating smaller design components allows for much greater flexibility and iterative design.
